# F7 Adjacent-System Note: Another University

## Self-description

The university system describes itself as a place where knowledge is
created, preserved, taught, and applied for public benefit.  Open
research is the current iteration of the claim that this process should
be transparent, reproducible, and collectively governed rather than
privately hoarded.  The UKRN Open Research Programme is one attempt to
make this operational across ~25 UK institutions through a
Train-the-Trainer model, funded by Research England at £7.5M over five
years.

## Problem it thinks exists

The stated problem is that open research practices are not embedded in
routine institutional culture despite widespread policy support.  The
intervention theory is that training intermediaries centrally, who then
deliver locally, will close the gap between policy aspiration and daily
practice.

## What it coordinates

In principle: knowledge, teaching, materials, evaluation, institutional
policy, recognition, and career incentives — across autonomous
institutions with very different governance structures, resource levels,
and cultures.

In practice: the coordination actually attempted is much thinner.  The
programme coordinates training content, a Community of Practice, and a
survey instrument.  The institutional coordination that would make the
training stick — workload models, stewardship, contribution pathways,
cross-institutional feedback — is assumed rather than designed.

## Memory model

Weak.  Individual trainers remember what they learned and what they
adapted.  The programme remembers headcounts and optional survey
responses.  Local adaptations are not systematically captured or
upstreamed.  Cross-institutional learning does not accumulate anywhere.
There is no durable shared memory of what was tried, what worked, and
what failed.  The evaluation workstream was funded but the memory
infrastructure was not built.

## Control model

Nominal hierarchy (central programme → institutional leads → ORCA
coordinators → trainers → recipients) but in practice control is
diffuse and unmonitored.  The central programme publishes materials and
runs training events.  What happens after that is up to the institution,
the coordinator, and the individual trainer — most of whom operate
without clear expectations, protected time, or feedback.  Control
dissipates at each handoff.

## Evidence model

Almost absent at the cross-institutional level.  Individual trainers
rely on optional post-session surveys, informal feedback, and
headcounts.  No one performs cross-institutional analysis.  The
programme cannot demonstrate impact to the institutions it depends on
for support.  This is the most coordination-intensive requirement and
the most consistently absent.

## Human role

The human is not a supervisor, collaborator, or consumer.  The human is
a *carrier* — an individual who absorbs a structural gap, performs four
to six roles that the system design distributes across distinct actors,
and sustains the programme through goodwill that will not survive their
departure.  This is the "absorbed input" mode: the practice survives as
long as the person persists.

## Nearest FUTON layer(s)

Multiple resonances:

- **F3 (coordination):** The five patterns are coordination problems.
  Confident Adaptation is specification clarity.  Protected Interaction
  is provisioning.  Shared Maintenance is commons stewardship.
  Contribution Pathways is collective-choice governance.  Visible Impact
  is monitoring.  These map directly to futon3/library concerns.

- **F7 (contextual):** The university is the context FUTON exists
  inside.  Understanding why the university fails to coordinate is
  understanding the pressure that makes FUTON's own coordination
  discipline non-arbitrary.

- **equity.tex:** The contribution→stake→governance→capability loop
  from equity.tex is the deep structure.  Every failure in the UKRN
  case is a broken joint in that loop.

## Five patterns mapped to the equity loop

| Pattern | Equity loop joint | What breaks |
|---|---|---|
| Confident Adaptation | Contribution → Stake | People contribute but don't know what counts.  Contribution without recognition. |
| Protected Interaction | Governance → Capability | Formal governance exists but doesn't improve the conditions under which people work.  Governance without capability expansion. |
| Shared Maintenance | Capability → Contribution | People adapt materials but can't upstream improvements.  Local capability that fragments instead of feeding back. |
| Contribution Pathways | Contribution → Stake → Governance | Contributors have no recognised scope to shape the system.  Contribution without voice. |
| Visible Impact | Feedback (whole loop) | No one can tell whether the loop is working.  The system cannot learn from its own variation. |

This is not a coincidence.  The five patterns *are* the equity loop
inspected at five different joints.  equity.tex provides the general
theory; the UKRN case provides the worked example; the futon3 patterns
provide the formal structure.

## Key parallel to FUTON

FUTON's core thesis is that coordination must be evidence-bearing,
pattern-mediated, and self-describing.  The university case shows what
happens when coordination is none of those things: evidence doesn't
accumulate, patterns are discovered locally and lost, and the system
cannot describe its own functioning to itself.  The five patterns are
what FUTON's coordination discipline would look like if applied to an
institutional rather than a computational context.

## Key non-parallel

FUTON can redesign its own coordination architecture.  The university
cannot — or rather, the people with the energy to redesign it don't
have the standing, and the people with the standing don't have the
energy.  This is the structural asymmetry that makes "another university
is possible — but not here" a statement about governance, not about
geography.

Academics are frequently too busy to get involved with creative ideas.
Goodwill is distributed in a linear correlation with existing
credentials.  Innovation is welcomed in principle and starved in
practice.  The people who carry the programme — the absorbed-input
trainers, the undertrained ORCAs, the librarians who felt "only
accidentally included" — are precisely the people whose contribution
creates no recognised stake.

This reactivates a punk/DIY sensibility: if the institution will not
close the loop, you build the loop elsewhere.  Not from the ashes of
the university, but alongside it — a parallel structure that takes the
equity criterion seriously because it is small enough to actually govern
itself.

## What FUTON can learn from it

1. **Coordination gradients are real.**  The five-pattern gradient
   (1-actor → 6-actor chain) is empirically grounded.  FUTON should
   expect similar gradients in its own coordination architecture and
   design for them explicitly.

2. **Memory infrastructure must be designed, not assumed.**  The UKRN
   programme funded an entire evaluation workstream but did not build
   the memory chain that would make evaluation possible.  FUTON's
   insistence on durable evidence and explicit gates is justified by
   exactly this kind of failure.

3. **The equity loop is the right diagnostic.**  Every FUTON layer
   should be testable against the five equity questions from
   equity.tex.  If contributors don't gain stake, if governance doesn't
   expand capability, if feedback doesn't close — the layer is
   extractive regardless of its stated intentions.

4. **Absorbed input is the default mode.**  Any system that depends on
   individual goodwill rather than institutional architecture will
   produce absorbed input.  FUTON's design should assume this is what
   will happen unless explicitly prevented.

## What it helps justify about FUTON's existence

The university case demonstrates that well-intentioned, well-funded
institutional programmes fail when they coordinate activities without
coordinating the architecture that makes those activities productive.
FUTON exists because that failure is not specific to universities.  It
is the general form of the problem: systems that describe what will be
done but not the institutional arrangements that make it work.

FUTON's ~800 patterns are an attempt to specify those arrangements —
not as a checklist of activities but as a library of conditions that
must hold, stated as context–tension–resolution triples with explicit
failure modes.  The university case shows why that library is not
academic indulgence but practical necessity.

Another university is possible.  It looks like a contribution ecosystem
where the loop actually closes: where doing the work creates stake,
stake creates governance, governance expands capability, and capability
feeds back into richer contribution.  Whether that ecosystem is called a
university, a cooperative, a pattern library, or something else is less
important than whether it closes the loop.

Does this close the loop?
